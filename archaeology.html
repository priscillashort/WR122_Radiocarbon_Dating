Radiocarbon
Dating in Archaeology

The California Department of Parks and Recreation explains the reliance of archeology
on radiocarbon dating and explore the concept of relative dating vs absolute dating. The authors
explain that before the discovery of radiocarbon dating, archaeologists could only use relative dating to
try to date artifacts. This means that archaeologists would compare artifacts from the same area to try to
determine how they relate to each other. This comparison of artifacts was open to interpretation and had flaws,
such as animals moving artifacts to different locations. The advent of radiocarbon dating allowed archaeologists
to try to date an artifact solely by examining the artifact itself to yield a numerical age of the artifact. Because
relative dating methods were not sufficient for determining accurate dates of artifacts, archaeologists turned to
radiocarbon dating as a primary dating method in the field of Archaeology. Archaeologists can only use
radiocarbon dating to try to date objects supposed to be between 400 and 50,000 years old, due to the
nature of Carbon-14 decay. Not only do archaeologists use radiocarbon dating as a primary dating
method, they also use it to give dates to whole areas when no other dating method could yield
results. This means that radiocarbon dating is being used as both a primary dating
method and the basis for other more relative dating methods. Radiocarbon
dating has completely changed the way archaeologist look at the age
and timeline of artifacts. (See Source Here)
