Specific Details
Rachel Wood writes an article for The Conversation that reveals
how Radiocarbon dating works, its background, and some current studies
regarding radiocarbon dating. Wood explains that there are three isotopes of
Carbon: Carbon-12, Carbon-13, and Carbon-14. Isotopes are different forms of the same
element where a atom has the same number of protons but a different number of neutrons. The
lighter Carbon isotopes with fewer neutrons (Carbon-12 and Carbon-13) are stable, but the heavier
Carbon 14 isotope with more neutrons is unstable. Because of Carbon 14’s instability it decays over time.
To try to determine when a creature died, scientists must know the ratio of Carbon-12 to Carbon-14 in the
atmosphere at the time the creature died. Unfortunately, this ratio has fluctuated over time. Because the date
the creature died is not already known, it is impossible to know the Carbon ratio at the time of death. Wood
also explains that orgainic material can be sensitive to outside contamination. Because there is only about
0.0000000001% of Carbon 14 in today’s atmosphere, it is difficult for scientists to measure Carbon-14
at all, and it means that any contamination of Carbon-14 could drastically change the estimated age
of the material. Because of this obstacle scientists try remove Carbon from an organic material
believing that some of the Carbon could be a contaminant. However, it is very difficult
to determine how much of the Carbon is contamination and how much is part of the
original material. Wood explains that if even 1% of the Carbon in an organic
sample is from current organic material, then the age of the
sample could be changed by 10,000 years
