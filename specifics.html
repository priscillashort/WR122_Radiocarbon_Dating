Specific Details
Rachel Wood writes an article for The Conversation website that reveals
how Radiocarbon dating works, some of its background, and some of the current
studies regarding radiocarbon dating. Wood begins by explaining that there are three
known isotopes of Carbon: Carbon-12, Carbon-13, and Carbon-14. Isotopes are different
forms of the same element where the atom has the same number of protons but a different number
of neutrons. The lighter Carbon isotopes with fewer neutrons such as Carbon-12 and Carbon-13 are
stable, but the heavier Carbon 14 isotope with more neutrons is unstable. Because of Carbon 14’s
instability it decays to Nitrogen 14 over time. All creatures have the same ratio of Carbon-12 to Carbon-14
as the atmosphere through eating and breathing while the creature is alive. To try to determine when a creature
died, scientists must know the ratio of Carbon-12 to Carbon-14 in the atmosphere at the time the creature
died. Unfortunately, this atmospheric ratio has fluctuated over time. Because the date the creature died is
not already known, it is impossible to know the Carbon ratio at the time of death. To try to resolve this
issue, scientists use other dating methods such as dendrochronology to attempt to calibrate the
radiocarbon dating. Wood explains that orgainic material can be sensitive to outside
contamination. Because there is only about 0.0000000001% of Carbon 14 in today’s
atmosphere, it is difficult for scientists to measure Carbon-14 at all, and it means
that any contamination of Carbon-14 could drastically change the estimated
age of the material. Because of this obstacle scientists try remove
Carbon from an organic material believing that
some of the Carbon could be a contaminant. However, it is very difficult to determine how much of the Carbon that is present is contamination and how much is part of the original material. Wood explains that if even 1% of the Carbon in an organic sample is from current organic material, then the age of the sample could be changed by 10,000 years (See Source Here)
