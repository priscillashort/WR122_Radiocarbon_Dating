Half-Life Fluctuation
Alex Knapp with Forbes Magazine writes an article that reveals new
breakthroughs in science concerning the half-life of radioactive isotopes. Knapp
explains that scientists in the past have always regarded the half-life of radioactive isotopes
to be constant values. This is the basis of radiocarbon dating because the half-life of Carbon that
has been measured in the past is used as a constant value in radiocarbon dating. The values is use as a
constant in the mathematical formula that is intended to calculate the age of an organic material. Knapp
points out that the concept of the half-life as a constant value is one of the first things that a scientist learns
when studying radiocarbon dating, because this concept is the basis of the method. However, Knapp also
explains that scientists as Stanford and Purdue have discovered that this supposedly constant half-life can
vary based on several factors. Knapp claims that, “if physicists at Stanford and Purdue are correct in their
findings, the whole theory of constant radioactive decay rates could be thrown out the door” (Knapp).
The scientists discovered this discrepancy in the half-life of radioactive isotopes by trying to discover
a better way to generate random numbers. When generating a random number a constant value
and an unpredictable value is needed. When the scientists tried to use the half-life as the
supposed constant value, they discovered that the value was in fact, not a constant.
Knapp elaborates by explaining that the half-life, or decay rate, of a
radioactive material can be affected by solar neutrinos.
However, the limited knowledge concerning
solar neutrinos means that scientist have very limited knowledge about why the half-life of radioactive materials can be measured at varying values. Overall, scientists at Stanford and Purdue have discovered that the half-life of radioactive materials is not a constant, as it was thought, but they are unable to determine why. (See Source Here)
