Unusual Uses

A J T Jull writes an article for the Journal of Physics that explains some of
the uses of radiocarbon dating and the way Carbon-14 can be measured using an
Accelerator Mass Spectrometry machine. The article titled “Some interesting and exotic
applications of Carbon-14 dating by accelerator mass spectrometry” details how the AMS machine
works to try to detect trace amounts of Carbon-14 in a substance. The article also describes several ways
that radiocarbon dating has been used. Radiocarbon dating has been used to try to date several famous
artifacts such as the Shroud of Turin and the Dead Sea Scrolls. When radiocarbon dating is used to date these
types of artifacts it can sometimes shape the beliefs and ideas that surround the artifacts. Radiocarbon dating
is also used in Archaeology as its main basis. Since the discovery of radiocarbon dating in the 1950s it has
replaced most relative dating methods in Archaeology as the primary method of dating. Because of
Archaeology reliance on radiocarbon dating, the presumed dates for the origination of humans has been
adjusted by thousands of years. Radiocarbon dating has also been used to determine how long a
meteorite has been on the surface of the earth. Because Carbon-14 is accumulated by the
meteorites while in space, the decay of this Carbon-14 once on the earth’s surface is
measured to try to date when the meteorite fell to earth. Radiocarbon dating has
also been used to try to date landscape changes such as volcanic flows
and glacier recession. Radiocarbon dating has also been used
in the study of tree rings to show changes in
solar activity over time.
These changes is solar activity such as solar flairs can cause variations in the cosmic-rays of the sun, which in turn can effect the amount of Carbon-14 present in the atmosphere. (See Source Here)
