Unusual Uses

A J T Jull, with the Journal of Physics, explains some interesting uses of
radiocarbon dating and that an Accelerator Mass Spectrometry machine can be used
to detect trace amounts of Carbon-14 in a substance. Radiocarbon dating has been used to
try to date several famous artifacts such as the Shroud of Turin and the Dead Sea Scrolls. When
radiocarbon dating is used to date these types of artifacts it can sometimes shape the beliefs and ideas
that surround the artifacts. Radiocarbon dating is also used in Archaeology as its main basis. Since the
discovery of radiocarbon dating in the 1940s it has replaced the relative dating methods in Archaeology as
the primary method of dating. Because of Archaeology's reliance on radiocarbon dating, the presumed dates
for the origination of humans has been adjusted by thousands of years. Radiocarbon dating has also been
used to try to determine how long a meteorite has been on the surface of the earth. Because Carbon-14 is
accumulated by the meteorites while in space, the decay of this Carbon-14 once on the earthâ€™s surface
is measured to try to date when the meteorite fell to earth. Radiocarbon dating has been used to
try to date landscape changes such as volcanic flows and glacier recession. Radiocarbon dating
has also been used in the study of tree rings to try to show changes in solar activity over
time. Jull points out that these changes in solar activity such as solar flairs can
cause variations in the cosmic-rays of the sun, which in turn can effect
the amount of Carbon-14 present in the atmosphere.
