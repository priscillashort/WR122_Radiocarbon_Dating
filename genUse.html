History
and General Uses

The American Chemical Society commemorates Willard Libby and his discovery
of radiocarbon dating and explain some of the history and current uses of radiocarbon
dating in Archaeology and Geology. Radiocarbon dating transformed Archaeology because it was
the first absolute dating method discovered. This means that radiocarbon dating gave a proposed
numerical age to an artifact rather than relying on relative dating methods. Relative dating methods were
only able to compare artifacts to other artifacts with “known” dates to try to arrive at a date for the original
artifact. This relative dating method was somewhat subjective and only yielded estimated dates. Radiocarbon
dating was supposed to give a much more accurate date because it is based on a principle of chemistry, and
therefore archaeologists adopted the method as their primary dating method. Radiocarbon is also used in
Geology even though the method cannot be directly used to date rocks because rocks do not contain
Carbon-14 the same way that organic organisms do. Geologists have attempted to use radiocarbon dating
to date tree samples buried under glacial ice. This use of radio Carbon dating in Geology has changed
the proposed timeline for some glacial movements by 13,000 to 15,000 years. These drastic
changes in timelines are consistent in both the archaeological and geological fields. The
authors propose that radiocarbon dating has been used in multiple fields to
“disprove” many believes that were prevalent at the time and as a
basis of many current views concerning age and
timeline. (See Source Here)
