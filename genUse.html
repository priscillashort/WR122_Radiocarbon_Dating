History
and General Uses

The authors of the American Chemical Society website write a commemorative
article about Willard Libby and his discovery of radiocarbon dating. The article explains
some of the history and current uses of radiocarbon dating. In the section of the article titled “The
‘Radiocarbon Revolution’” the authors elaborate on how radiocarbon dating is used in Archaeology
and Geology. Radiocarbon dating transformed Archaeology because it was the first absolute dating
method discovered. This means that radiocarbon dating gave a proposed numerical age to an artifact rather
than relying on relative dating methods. Relative dating methods were only able to compare artifacts to other
artifacts with “known” dates to try to arrive at a date for the original artifact. This relative dating method was
somewhat subjective and only yielded estimates on the dates of the artifacts. Radiocarbon dating was
supposed to give a much more accurate date to the artifacts because it is based on a principle of chemistry,
and therefore archaeologists adopted the method as their primary dating method. Radiocarbon is also
used in Geology even though the method cannot be directly used to date rocks because rocks do
not contain Carbon-14 the same way that organic organisms do. Geologists have attempted
to use radiocarbon dating to date tree samples buried under glacial ice. This use of
radio Carbon dating in Geology has changed the proposed timeline for the
recession of glacial ice in North America by 13,000 to 15,000 years.
These drastic changes in timelines are consistent in
both the archaeological and geological fields. The authors propose that radiocarbon dating has been used in multiple fields to “disprove” many believes that were prevalent at the time. Radiocarbon dating is used as a basis of many current views concerning age and timeline. (See Source Here)
