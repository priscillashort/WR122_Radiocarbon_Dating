The Do's and Don'ts of Writing
Priscilla Short

Overview





















Do's

When conducting research, it is important to gather lots of information quickly and then sort through it later. If you analyze each source as you find it, you will lose track of the main idea and get lost and discouraged. One trick that I use is to open a word doc and call it sources, I then open a browser and type in a basic web search of my topic. I click on links that look like they could work for my paper and then read the first paragraph and check the length of the source. If I have not eliminated the source as an option after reading the first paragraph, I add it to my source document and move on to the next source. If my topic is somewhat dynamic, or I already have an idea of how I want to break the paper into sections, then I will add a brief header explaining each section of sources. For example, I used “basic radiocarbon dating info”, “flaws”, and “uses” as headers in my sources document. By conducting research in this way, you can quickly get a big picture of your topic and glean lots of information that can be used in your paper later. (Go to top)

When drafting your paper or a bibliography I would recommend writing your thoughts as they come, without editing. This brain dump type of writing can be really helpful when trying to write a coherent and well-structured paper. If you are able to see the big picture of your topic and you write those thoughts quickly without dealing with verbiage and syntax, then you usually are able to relate each part of the paper to the overarching theme better. If you get caught up in the details, then you can lose track of the main idea, and the sections won’t flow together as well. I find it most helpful when I am able to write the entire paper draft, or at least a major section, in one sitting. After drafting the paper, or a section, you can go back and analyze your verbiage, grammar, and sentence structure. (Go to top)

When you start to write your paper draft, I would recommend summarizing each source you plan to use. Your teacher may already have you do this in an annotated bibliography, but if he/she doesn’t require that you do this, I would still highly recommend this step. If you summarize what your source is saying in your own words, you will often emphasize the parts of the source that relate to your paper. If you have a list of summaries of your sources, these paragraphs can usually be put directly into your paper, and then edited to fit their position in the paper. By writing this way, you can save yourself a lot of mental work, while enhancing the credibility of your paper. Usually when you choose a source, it is because the source is making a point that you already want to emphasize in your paper. If you can use their ideas, and just change the wording to emphasize your point, then you have saved yourself a lot of work. Just remember to be careful to paraphrase and summarize carefully and cite your sources accurately. (Go to top)



Don'ts

If possible, avoid using the Microsoft Word app for Chromebooks. I used this app for much of my paper and annotated bibliography and lost a lot of data. The app is unpredictable and can crash unexpectedly. Even if you are using the adage, “save early and save often”, you can still lose data. During the process of writing my annotated bibliography, my Word app crashed. I thought it wouldn’t be a big deal because I had saved recently. Unfortunately, I tried to open the file and it had been corrupted. None of the tools available online could recover the data and I lost about 11 pages of writing. This was not a onetime occurrence as it happened with other documents on other occasions. If you must use the Microsoft Word app for Chromebook, I would recommend doing a save as rather than just a save very often to avoid losing data. (Go to top)

Try to avoid overanalyzing your sources. It is important to know the main point of each source, but if you read every word meticulously, then you are going to get bogged down. Once I have reached the point in my project where I am summarizing the sources, I will re-read the first paragraph, read the last paragraph, and skim the rest. I am usually looking for key words that relate to my topic, and then I read the surrounding paragraph. If you have gathered lots of sources quickly without reading them (as I would recommend), then you should find that a lot of the sources you have, cannot be used. As long as you have gathered enough sources in the research stage, you should still be left with a decent sized pool of resources. Just remember that your goal is to convey an overarching conglomeration of ideas to emphasize a central idea, not become an expert in any field overnight. (Go to top)
