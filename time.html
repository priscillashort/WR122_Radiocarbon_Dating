Fluctuations Over Time

Andrea Cohn at MSU exposes some main flaws of radiocarbon dating, including
the fluctuating amount of Carbon in the atmosphere over time, the restrictive nature of the
Carbon half-life, and the inability to determine when a material was last used as a tool. Cohn
explains the fluctuation of Carbon in the atmosphere by saying, “Unfortunately, the amount of
Carbon-14 in the atmosphere has not been steady throughout history. In fact, it has fluctuated a great
deal over the years. This variation is caused by both natural processes and human activity” (Cohn). This
fluctuation of Carbon in the atmosphere can drastically affect the results of radiocarbon dating. Not only
has there been fluctuation, there has also sudden changes to the amount of Carbon-14 in the atmosphere.
Cohn states that, “the quantity of Carbon-14 was nearly doubled in the ’50s and ’60s because of the
atomic bomb testings [sic] in those decades” (Cohn). These drastic changes, as well as the more
minor fluctuations, can have a dramatic impact on the results of radiocarbon dating brcause
the ratio of carbon isotopes in the atmosphere is used as a constant value in radiocarbon
dating. If these fluctuations are not known and accounted for, the results of the
radiocarbon dating can be off by 10% to 20% (Cohn). (See Source Here)
