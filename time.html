Andrea Cohn writes an article as a student at MSU while studying in the field
of anthropology. Cohn writes concerning some flaws in the radiocarbon dating method.
This is a brief article that explains the basic concept of radiocarbon dating and how it is used to
date organic materials. Cohn also explains three main flaws of radiocarbon dating, including the
fluctuating amount of Carbon in the atmosphere over time, the restricted date range when the method is
applicable, and the inability to determine when a piece of wood was last used as a tool. Cohn explains that
the amount of Carbon in the atmosphere has fluctuated considerably over time. Cohn states, “Unfortunately,
the amount of Carbon-14 in the atmosphere has not been steady throughout history. In fact, it has fluctuated
a great deal over the years. This variation is caused by both natural processes and human activity” (Cohn).
This fluctuation of Carbon in the atmosphere can affect the results of radiocarbon dating. Not only has
there been fluctuation, there has also been drastic and sudden changes to the amount of Carbon-14 in
the atmosphere. Cohn states that, “the quantity of Carbon-14 was nearly doubled in the ’50s and
’60s because of the atomic bomb testings [sic] in those decades.” (Cohn). These drastic
changes, as well as the more minor fluctuations, can have a dramatic impact on the
results of radiocarbon dating. Cohn explains that if these fluctuations are not
known and accounted for the results of the radiocarbon dating can be
off by 10% to 20% (Cohn). Another flaw in radiocarbon
dating that Cohn presents is the fact that
radiocarbon dating can only be used for materials supposed to be 63,000 years old or newer. Radiocarbon dating cannot be used for materials older than this because all the Carbon-14 would have decayed by this time and there is no longer a ratio that can be measured. This means that there is a relatively short period of time that this method would be effective compared to the proposed age of the earth. (See Source Here)
