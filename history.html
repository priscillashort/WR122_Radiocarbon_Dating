What is Radiocarbon Dating?






History and Math
Jay Noller et al, in a book about Geochronology, explains several dating
methods for both organic and inorganic materials. Noller provides technical
information about radiocarbon dating as it was understood in 2000. Noller explains that
because Carbon-14 is radioactive it spontaneously emits alpha particles and gamma rays. This
radioactive decay leads to the creation of a daughter element and the disintegration of the parent
element (Carbon-14). This process of decay occurrs all radioactive isotopes, and when these isotopes are
used for dating materials, the method is called an “isotopic" dating method (Noller p 3). In Noller’s table of
dating methods, he depicts isotopic dating as a method that yields a numerical age, rather than a relative, or
correlated age (Noller p 3). Noller further explains that the half-life of the parent element is the time it takes
for half of the parent atoms decay into daughter atoms (Noller p 6). At one point Noller states that “This
decay occurs at a constant rate without regard to the environmental factors” (Noller p 6). Noller also
gives the mathematical formulas used to calculate the age of a material. The formula takes into
account the amount of parent atoms in a given material, the amount of daughter atoms, the
estimated amount of the parent element at the time of death, and the decay rate or
half-life of the parent element. The half-life of the parent element is used as a
constant in the mathematical formula. This means that accuracy of the
formula is dependent on the idea that the half-life of Carbon-14
does not fluctuate. (See Source Here)
