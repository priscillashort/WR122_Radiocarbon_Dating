Carbon Fluctuations
Based on Region
Daniel Aloi with Phys.org exposes inaccuracies in radiocarbon dating concerning
different areas in the world. Aloi points out that there are different Carbon-14
measurements in different areas of the world. Aloi focuses on the Levant region, including
Israel, southern Jordan, and Egypt and explains that even a minor offset in Carbon-14 can have sizeable
impacts on the calculated age of an organic material. The amount of Carbon-14 in the atmosphere can vary
based on the time of the year and plants absorb Carbon-14 from the atmosphere at different times of the year
in different regions of the world. This means that if scientists expect the amount of Carbon-14 to be consistent
in all organic materials across the world, some ages derived through radiocarbon dating will be skewed. Aloi
points out that some scientists have argued to adjust the timeline of some artifacts because of these findings
but the proposed adjustments are not sufficient. Archaeologist, Sturt Manning, quoted in Aloi says “There
has been much debate for several decades among scholars arguing for different chronologies sometimes
only decades to a century apart—each with major historical implications. And yet these studies ...
may all be inaccurate since they are using the wrong radiocarbon information” (Aloi). Because
scientists are still using the wrong radiocarbon information, the ages derived from the
current radiocarbon dating method could be considerably skewed. Manning
suggests that Carbon-14 levels based on region warrants more research
and a serious look at how radiocarbon dating is used in the
scientific fields. (See Source Here)
