Daniel Aloi writes an article for Phys.org titled “Research Illuminates Inaccuracies
in Radiocarbon Dating” that exposes inaccuracies in the radiocarbon dating concerning
different areas in the world. The flaws Aloi describes are mostly because of the different Carbon-14
measurements in different areas of the world. Aloi focuses on the Levant region, including Israel,
southern Jordan, and Egypt. Aloi explains that even a minor offset in Carbon-14 can have sizeable impacts
on the calculated age of an organic material in question. Aloi explains that the amount of Carbon-14 in the
atmosphere can vary based on the time of the year. Plants absorb Carbon-14 from the atmosphere and they
grow at different times of the year in different parts of the world. This means that if scientists expect the
amount of Carbon-14 in an organic material to be consistent in all materials across the world, some ages of
materials derived through radiocarbon dating can be skewed. Aloi points out that some scientists have
argued to adjust the timeline of some artifacts because of these findings but the proposed adjustments
are not sufficient. Archaeologist, Sturt Manning, quoted in Aloi’s article says “There has been much
debate for several decades among scholars arguing for different chronologies sometimes only
decades to a century apart—each with major historical implications. And yet these
studies ... may all be inaccurate since they are using the wrong radiocarbon
information” (Aloi). Because scientists are still using the wrong
radiocarbon information, the results of ages derived
from the current radiocarbon dating
method could be considerably skewed. Manning further suggests that the findings concerning the Carbon-14 in different areas of the world warrant more research and a serious look at the way radiocarbon dating is used in Archaeology and other scientific fields. (See Source Here)
