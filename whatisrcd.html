What is Radiocarbon Dating?
Radiocarbon dating is used as a primary method for attempting to date
organic material. Because this method is used as a foundation for several theories
concerning the age of the earth and artifacts, the method should be thoroughly analyzed. After
analyzing radiocarbon dating, scientists have found several inconsistencies and flaws that warrant
extensive research. Some of the flaws of radiocarbon dating include, the fluctuating amount of
Carbon-14 in the atmosphere over time, the restricted date range when the method is applicable, and the
false assumption that the half-life of Carbon-14 is a constant value. Scientists should further evaluate
radiocarbon dating with a critical eye to determine if radiocarbon dating is reliable for accurately dating
organic material. If other methods can be found that prove to be more accurate dating methods than these
methods should be used instead, or in conjunction with radiocarbon dating. A recent method that was
devised to check the accuracy of radiocarbon dating on a case by case basis could open the door to
other dating methods and bring a middle ground to the scientific discussion of radiocarbon
dating.
