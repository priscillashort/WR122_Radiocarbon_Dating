Carbon-13 Test

Ben Panko writes on article for the Smithsonian Institution that warns against
the growing inaccuracies of radiocarbon dating. Panko explains that radiocarbon
dating has been drastically affected by fluctuation of the amount of Carbon-14 in the
atmosphere. The amount of Carbon in the atmosphere has changed drastically in recent years, partly
because of the burning of fossil fuels. Because of this drastic fluctuation, Panko explains that the
science of radiocarbon dating could become virtually obsolete within the next few decades. Some scientists
have predicted that, “By 2050, new samples of organic material will appear to have the same radiocarbon
date as samples from 1,000 years ago” (Panko). This change in the amount of Carbon in the atmosphere
especially threatens the accuracy of dating relatively recent materials because it makes the materials look
much older than they are. Panko also points out that radiocarbon dating has completely changed the field
of Archaeology and this dependence on radiocarbon dating is threatened. Panko presents the idea of
Peter Kohler to use Carbon-13 to determine if radiocarbon dating can be used on a particular organic
sample. If the amount of Carbon-13 in a sample is lower or higher than expected, it is determined
that the amount of Carbon-14 has also been skewed because of fluctuating Carbon-14
amounts in the atmosphere. When it is determined that the amount of Carbon-14
in the sample cannot be trusted, the door is opened for other dating methods
to be used to date the sample. Although this technique could be
useful in determining the accuracy of radiocarbon
dating, not all scientists want to use it to test all organic samples because they believe they already know the age of the sample. However, if this technique yields an extra check on the age of a material, it could prove invaluable, especially considering the flaws that have been discovered in the radiocarbon dating process.
